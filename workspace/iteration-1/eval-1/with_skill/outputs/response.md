## Steelman: Raising the federal minimum wage to $25/hour would be net positive for low-income workers

The core case rests on three mutually reinforcing pillars: purchasing power, monopsonistic labor markets, and the empirical record of recent large minimum wage increases.

**The purchasing power argument is underrated.** The federal minimum wage of $7.25/hour has lost roughly 40% of its real value since 1968. A worker earning $7.25 today commands less than a worker earning the equivalent wage fifty years ago. A $25 minimum wage — approximately what the 1968 minimum would be in 2024 dollars adjusted for productivity gains, not just inflation — would restore the historical relationship between minimum compensation and the overall productive capacity of the economy. Low-income workers spend a far higher share of their income than high earners, so wage increases at the bottom circulate rapidly back into local economies, generating demand that partially offsets any employment costs.

**Labor markets are not perfectly competitive — they are monopsonistic.** The standard "intro econ" objection to minimum wage hikes assumes competitive markets where wages reflect marginal product. But low-wage labor markets are structurally monopsonistic: workers have limited mobility (due to transportation costs, geographic ties, childcare constraints), face information asymmetries, and are subject to coordination failures that suppress wages below the competitive equilibrium. In a monopsonistic market, a minimum wage floor doesn't destroy jobs — it corrects the power imbalance, potentially *increasing* both wages and employment simultaneously. The concentration of low-wage employment in sectors like fast food, retail, and logistics — dominated by large chains with pricing power — is precisely the market structure where monopsony effects are strongest.

**The empirical evidence from large increases supports limited disemployment.** The Seattle Minimum Wage Study (Dube et al.), the work of Arindrajit Dube across multiple datasets, and analysis of state-level minimum wage variation consistently find small or statistically insignificant employment effects even for moderate-to-large increases. California and New York City have implemented wages well above $15 with no observable employment catastrophe. More importantly, the research consistently finds that total earnings among low-wage workers increase even when some hours are reduced — workers are better off in aggregate. The distributional evidence is particularly strong: minimum wage increases are among the most precisely targeted anti-poverty tools available, with benefits flowing overwhelmingly to workers in the bottom two income quintiles.

**The knock-on effects compound over time.** Higher wages reduce turnover (reducing employer training costs), reduce reliance on public assistance programs (reducing fiscal burden), and may increase worker productivity through efficiency wage effects. The net public cost is therefore lower than the gross wage increase suggests.

The steelmanned claim: a $25 federal minimum wage would raise the living standards of roughly 33 million workers, correct for decades of real wage suppression at the bottom, and do so in labor market conditions structurally suited to absorb the increase with limited disemployment.

## Red Team: Against the Steelman

**The $25 number is not calibrated to labor market conditions — it's a political round number, and the magnitude matters enormously.** The empirical record supporting minimum wage increases is drawn almost entirely from studies of increases to $10, $12, or $15 — not $25. A $25 federal minimum wage would represent a 245% increase over the current federal floor. The gap between studied policy and proposed policy is not a detail; it is the crux. Extrapolating from studies of moderate increases to a more-than-tripling of the minimum is methodologically unsound. Nonlinearities are real: small increases may be absorbed; large ones may not be.

**The geographic heterogeneity problem is fatal to the federal framing.** Median wages vary dramatically across the US. In rural Mississippi or West Virginia, a $25 minimum wage would compress nearly the entire wage distribution — it would exceed the median wage in many counties. The labor markets that would face the most disruptive impact are precisely those with the least capacity to absorb it: low-productivity regions, small businesses operating on thin margins, rural economies with no competitive pressure from alternative employers. The case for monopsony is strongest in urban, high-cost areas; it is weakest in rural low-wage regions. A federal floor at $25 forces conditions appropriate to San Francisco onto economies that structurally cannot bear them.

**The monopsony argument proves too much.** If monopsony power is so pervasive and severe that wages are dramatically below competitive equilibrium, then we should observe large employer profits in low-wage industries. But fast food and retail operate on 2-5% net margins. Where is the monopsony rent? If employers aren't capturing large surplus, there is no room to compress margins to fund a wage increase — the adjustment must come through reduced employment, reduced hours, accelerated automation, or price increases that partially offset wage gains for low-income consumers.

**Automation risk is underweighted in the empirical literature.** Most studies of minimum wage effects look at short-run employment. Automation investment has long lags — firms don't immediately install kiosks and robotic fryers, they do so over years as capital costs fall and labor costs rise. The long-run employment effects of a $25 wage may be substantially more negative than the short-run studies capture. McDonald's has already deployed automated ordering; warehouse automation is advancing rapidly. A $25 federal minimum would dramatically accelerate the business case for replacing low-wage workers, with effects falling hardest on the least-skilled workers the policy is meant to help.

**"Net positive for low-income workers" is doing a lot of work.** If 30 million workers receive higher wages and 3 million lose jobs, is that net positive? The aggregate wage bill may increase while the employment-poor — who are often disproportionately young, less-educated, minority, or recently entered the workforce — bear concentrated losses. The workers who lose jobs or hours are not randomly distributed; they are the most marginal workers, often those the policy is nominally most concerned with protecting.

## Steelman: A $25 federal minimum wage would be net negative for low-income workers

The strongest counter-case is not "markets are efficient so don't interfere." It is: even accepting that intervention is warranted, a blunt federal floor at $25 is the wrong tool, and its costs fall most heavily on the workers it claims to protect.

**The geographic mismatch argument is decisive on its own.** The US is not one labor market. It is hundreds of overlapping regional economies with vastly different wage structures, productivity levels, and cost-of-living profiles. A $25 minimum in rural Alabama is not the same policy as a $25 minimum in Manhattan — it is a qualitatively different intervention. In low-wage regions, a $25 floor would immediately exceed the median wage, eliminating the wage gradient that allows workers to be hired, trained, and promoted into higher-productivity roles. The result would be mass unemployment in the exact communities most economically vulnerable, with no local labor market mechanism for adjustment.

**The low-skill penalty compounds over time.** A minimum wage creates a floor, but it also creates a barrier to entry. Employers who must pay $25/hour will not hire workers whose productivity they estimate below $25/hour. This is not hypothetical cruelty — it is basic rational behavior under uncertainty. The workers most likely to fall below the productivity threshold are: young workers with no experience, workers with criminal records, workers with limited English proficiency, workers with disabilities, workers returning to the workforce after gaps. These are not abstract categories — they are disproportionately minority workers and workers from the lowest income backgrounds. The minimum wage, when set high enough, functions as a credential barrier that excludes the least advantaged from the formal labor market entirely, pushing them into the informal economy or into permanent unemployment.

**The fiscal offsetting argument cuts against the claim.** If a $25 minimum wage significantly reduces employment among low-income workers, they lose wages but also lose employer-provided benefits, and their remaining income from government transfer programs may not fully compensate. A worker who was earning $15,000/year from part-time low-wage work and supplementing with SNAP, Medicaid, and housing assistance is not obviously better off unemployed with full benefits — both because the combination of work and benefits provided more total resources, and because employment provides non-monetary goods (structure, skill accumulation, social connection) that transfer programs do not replace.

**The better policy exists.** The strongest version of the counter-claim is not "do nothing" — it is "there are better-targeted tools." The Earned Income Tax Credit, negative income tax proposals, and wage subsidies can raise low-income worker take-home pay without creating the employment floor that harms the most marginal workers. If the goal is to put more money in the hands of low-income workers, the EITC has a strong empirical track record of doing exactly that without the employment destruction risk. Choosing a $25 federal minimum wage over an expanded EITC is a policy choice with real victims.

## Red Team: Against the Counter-Claim's Steelman

**The geographic heterogeneity argument proves too much — it proves that there should be no federal minimum wage, not that $25 is wrong.** The current $7.25 federal minimum also creates regional mismatches; Mississippi's labor market is different from New York's under the current law too. If the argument is that federal floors are always inappropriate because of geographic variation, that's a case against the minimum wage institution entirely, not against a specific level. If the argument is only about magnitude, then it concedes the principle and becomes an empirical dispute about what level is right — which is a much weaker position.

**The "low-skill penalty" argument assumes a fixed productivity hierarchy.** The concern that workers below the $25 productivity threshold get excluded treats productivity as a fixed characteristic of workers rather than a function of investment, training, and institutional context. When wages are higher, employers invest more in training (because the worker is worth more), workers invest more in skill acquisition (because returns are higher), and the productivity threshold shifts upward. The empirical literature on high-minimum-wage regimes in Scandinavia and parts of Germany does not show the permanent underclass exclusion effects the theory predicts. Tight labor markets with high floors can compress the productivity distribution upward.

**The EITC-as-alternative argument has a well-documented flaw.** The EITC is funded by general taxpayers, including low-income workers, while the benefits of a minimum wage are paid by employers. The EITC can function as a subsidy to low-wage employers — when the government tops up wages, employers can offer lower wages than they otherwise would, capturing part of the subsidy as higher profit. There is empirical evidence this effect is real. A minimum wage forces employers to pay a floor; an EITC lets them free-ride on the public subsidy. For workers, an EITC expansion without a minimum wage floor may be partially captured by employer wage suppression.

**The "automation" objection proves too much across time.** Every labor cost increase in history has prompted predictions of mass technological unemployment — from the mechanization of agriculture to ATMs to containerization. These predictions have consistently proven wrong in aggregate, though they were right about specific jobs. The issue is not whether some jobs get automated, but whether new jobs emerge to replace them and whether the gains to workers in surviving jobs outweigh the losses to displaced workers. The historical record is, at minimum, not a slam-dunk for the "automation catastrophe" prediction.

## Synthesis

**What survived red-teaming:**

From the steelman side: The monopsony correction argument and the purchasing power argument survived in weakened form. There is genuine evidence that labor markets are not perfectly competitive and that moderate minimum wage increases do not produce large disemployment effects. The empirical record through $15/hour is relatively solid.

From the counter-claim side: The geographic heterogeneity argument survived substantially. A $25 federal floor would create genuinely different conditions in rural low-wage economies than in high-cost urban markets. The magnitude question — whether the empirical evidence at $12-15 extrapolates to $25 — also survived. The red team on the counter-claim weakened the EITC argument (employer capture) and the productivity-floor argument (productivity is malleable), but it did not eliminate the core geographic concern.

**What converges:**

Both sides implicitly agree that the harm of minimum wage increases is most likely to fall on the most marginal workers — the youngest, least-skilled, and most economically precarious. The steelman minimizes this risk; the counter-claim maximizes it. Both sides also agree that the existing federal minimum is indefensibly low. The dispute is about magnitude and mechanism, not principle.

**What genuinely contradicts:**

The core contradiction is an unresolved empirical question: does the labor market evidence from moderate increases ($10-$15) extend to a $25 federal floor? This depends on whether disemployment effects are linear or nonlinear in the size of the increase, and no strong empirical evidence resolves this because the policy has not been implemented at this scale nationally. It also depends on geographic labor market structure: in monopsonistic urban markets, $25 may clear; in competitive rural markets, it likely will not.

**The reasoning chain:**

1. The steelman is strong on the mechanism (monopsony correction, real wage recovery) and on the evidence for moderate increases. It weakens significantly on the $25 threshold specifically and on geographic heterogeneity.

2. The red team on the steelman lands hardest on magnitude extrapolation and geographic applicability — these are the genuinely damaging objections.

3. The counter-claim steelman is strongest on the geographic mismatch and on the most-marginal-worker exclusion concern. It is weaker than it appears on the EITC alternative (due to employer capture effects) and on automation timing.

4. The red team on the counter-claim partially rehabilitates the steelman by showing the productivity argument is not as fixed as assumed and that the EITC alternative has its own problems. But it does not overcome the geographic heterogeneity problem.

**Final verdict:**

The claim as stated — that a $25 federal minimum wage would be *net positive* for low-income workers — is probably false, and the falseness is not close-call. The key failure is the "federal" framing combined with the $25 number. The empirical case for minimum wage increases is meaningful but is built on evidence from more modest increases and from urban labor markets where the increases are most defensible. A $25 federal floor would impose conditions calibrated for high-cost urban markets onto hundreds of rural and lower-productivity regional economies, producing concentrated disemployment among the most marginal workers in those areas.

If the claim were "raising minimum wages substantially above the current $7.25 federal floor would be net positive for most low-income workers in high-cost labor markets," the answer would likely be yes. If the claim were "a phased, regionally differentiated approach reaching $25 in high-cost metros but lower elsewhere would be net positive," the evidence is more supportive. But a uniform $25 federal floor applied to the full geographic diversity of US labor markets is a blunt instrument that, at that magnitude, is more likely to harm than help the most economically vulnerable workers — the ones least able to meet the productivity threshold it sets.

The honest bottom line: the direction is right, the mechanism is partially right, the number and the mechanism of implementation are wrong.
