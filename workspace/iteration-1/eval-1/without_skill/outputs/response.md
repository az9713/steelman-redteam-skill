# Steelman and Red Team: Raising the Minimum Wage to $25/Hour Would Be Net Positive for Low-Income Workers

---

## STEELMAN: The Strongest Case FOR

### 1. The Current Federal Minimum Wage Has Lost Enormous Real Value

The federal minimum wage has been $7.25/hour since 2009 — the longest stretch without an increase in US history. Adjusted for inflation, the purchasing power of the minimum wage peaked in 1968 at roughly $13–14 in today's dollars. A $25 floor would not be radical; it would be a meaningful restoration of purchasing power that workers have been steadily losing for decades. Low-income workers have effectively received a sustained pay cut through inflation year after year.

### 2. Low-Wage Workers Have a High Marginal Propensity to Consume

Economists broadly agree that lower-income households spend a much higher share of additional income compared to wealthy households. A significant wage increase funnels money directly into local economies — groceries, rent, childcare, transportation — generating multiplier effects that stimulate demand and can partially offset employer cost increases. The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) and others have modeled scenarios where aggregate consumer demand rises enough to support some of the jobs that might otherwise be lost.

### 3. Empirical Evidence from State-Level Increases Is Encouraging

Over the past decade, more than two dozen states have raised their minimum wages significantly above the federal level, and several cities (Seattle, San Francisco, New York) have phased in $15–$20+ floors. Extensive academic research — including influential work by economists Dube, Reich, and Zipperer — finds that moderate-to-significant minimum wage increases in these jurisdictions did not produce the large employment losses predicted by simple supply-and-demand models. Workers' incomes rose, poverty rates fell, and restaurant and retail employment remained broadly stable in most cases.

### 4. Monopsony Power Suppresses Wages Below Market-Clearing Levels

Classical economics assumes competitive labor markets, but low-wage labor markets are often characterized by monopsony (employer-side market power). Large employers like Amazon, Walmart, and fast food chains can set wages below competitive levels because workers face high switching costs, limited local alternatives, and information asymmetries. When markets are monopsonistic, raising the minimum wage can actually increase both employment and wages simultaneously — it forces wages closer to the true competitive equilibrium. This is not a fringe view; it appears in mainstream labor economics journals and has influenced Federal Reserve thinking.

### 5. Reduces Reliance on Public Assistance — a Hidden Subsidy Paid by Taxpayers

A substantial share of fast food workers, Walmart employees, and other low-wage workers rely on SNAP, Medicaid, and housing assistance even while employed full-time. Estimates suggest that taxpayers spend tens of billions of dollars annually subsidizing wages at large, profitable corporations. Raising the minimum wage shifts this cost back to employers and shareholders, reduces the social safety net burden, and reduces the implicit taxpayer subsidy to low-wage business models.

### 6. Reduces Inequality and Its Associated Harms

The US has among the highest levels of income inequality in the developed world. Research consistently links high inequality to worse health outcomes, lower social mobility, higher crime rates, and lower life satisfaction. A higher wage floor directly compresses the bottom of the income distribution. For low-income workers specifically, the wage gain is not merely financial — it translates into dignity, reduced financial stress, better health outcomes, and more stable family environments.

### 7. Long Phase-In Periods Allow Adjustment

No serious proposal calls for an overnight jump to $25. A 5–7 year phased increase gives businesses time to adjust prices, improve productivity, automate selectively, and absorb costs gradually. Inflation distributes some of the cost adjustment across the economy broadly. A long runway dramatically reduces the risk of disruptive employment shocks.

### 8. The Counterfactual Is Not Zero Cost

Critics focus on potential job losses from a wage increase, but they rarely account for the significant harms of the status quo: chronic poverty, high worker turnover (which imposes substantial costs on employers), workers holding multiple jobs with limited time for family or civic life, and the drag on consumer demand from an economy where wages at the bottom have stagnated for generations. The policy question is not "wage floor vs. perfect market" but "wage floor vs. continued stagnation."

---

## RED TEAM: The Strongest Case AGAINST

### 1. $25/Hour Is Economically Disconnected from Most of the Country

The US is not one labor market. A $25/hour minimum in San Francisco or Manhattan may be reasonable. In rural Mississippi, West Virginia, or the Texas panhandle, the median wage for many occupations is only marginally above that level. Imposing a single national floor calibrated to high-cost metros effectively destroys the economic viability of entire categories of employment in low-cost regions. The CBO's 2021 analysis of a $15 federal minimum estimated 1.4 million job losses nationally; scaling to $25 could produce losses several times larger, concentrated in exactly the regions with the most vulnerable low-wage workers.

### 2. The Employment Destruction Will Disproportionately Harm the Most Vulnerable

Minimum wage increases do not affect all low-income workers equally. The workers most likely to lose jobs or hours are those with the least labor market power: teenagers entering the workforce for the first time, workers with criminal records, workers without high school diplomas, undocumented workers, and workers in sectors with thin margins. These workers may be precisely the ones who need a foothold in the labor market most. A policy that raises wages for those who keep their jobs while pricing others out entirely may be a regressive redistribution within the low-income population.

### 3. Small Businesses Cannot Absorb the Shock the Way Large Corporations Can

Walmart and Amazon can absorb a wage increase, automate, or pass costs to consumers with relative ease. A family-owned restaurant, small retailer, or local childcare provider has no such cushion. Profit margins in food service often run 3–5%. A 50–150% labor cost increase — even phased in — could be existential for many small businesses. The outcome could be market consolidation that leaves large corporations even more dominant in low-wage labor markets, with fewer employers competing for workers over time.

### 4. Automation Acceleration Could Eliminate More Jobs Than It Saves

Wage increases do not just reduce headcount — they dramatically accelerate the business case for automation. Kiosk ordering, automated inventory, robotic food preparation, self-checkout, and AI-driven customer service are already commercially available. At $7.25/hour, the payback period on a $50,000 kiosk system is long. At $25/hour, it becomes very short. The result is not simply that some workers earn more — it is that the categories of work available to low-skill workers shrink permanently, making future re-entry into the labor market harder.

### 5. Inflation Can Erode the Real Wage Gains

A significant minimum wage increase affects a large share of the US workforce and cost structures across the service economy. Businesses will raise prices. When low-income workers spend more at these same businesses, the real purchasing power gain is partially offset. If inflation is broad-based, workers on fixed incomes (elderly, disabled) who are not minimum wage earners may be made worse off. The net real wage gain for minimum wage workers may be substantially smaller than the nominal increase implies.

### 6. The Empirical Literature Does Not Clearly Generalize to $25

Studies showing limited employment effects typically examine increases in the range of $1–$4/hour, often in local areas with already-higher median wages. Extrapolating these findings to a more than tripling of the federal minimum wage is a significant leap. The underlying economics are non-linear — a 10% wage increase and a 200% wage increase are categorically different in terms of adjustment pressure. The research base that steelman advocates cite does not actually study increases of this magnitude.

### 7. It May Reduce Hours and Benefits More Than It Reduces Headcount

Employers facing higher labor costs have multiple margins of adjustment beyond layoffs: cutting hours (moving workers from 35 hours to 28 hours to avoid benefit thresholds), reducing non-wage benefits (employer-provided health insurance, paid leave, meals), intensifying work pace, or eliminating break time and flexibility. Many of these adjustments are hard to observe in aggregate employment data. A worker who keeps their job but loses employer-subsidized health insurance and sees their hours cut by 20% may be worse off in total compensation even as the headline wage rises.

### 8. Timing and Economic Context Matter Enormously

A $25 minimum wage imposed during a period of high inflation, rising interest rates, or recession could have far worse employment effects than the same policy during a period of strong growth and tight labor markets. Locking in a specific dollar figure nationally, regardless of economic conditions or regional variation, removes the flexibility that good policy requires. A wage floor that makes sense in 2026 conditions may be highly disruptive in a recession or in a future era of rapid AI-driven labor displacement.

### 9. It May Reduce Work Incentives at the Margin

A $25/hour minimum wage intersects with the structure of means-tested benefit programs in complex ways. Some low-income workers who rely on SNAP, Medicaid, housing vouchers, and EITC may face implicit marginal tax rates well above 50% once these benefits phase out. For some workers — particularly those with childcare costs — the net gain from earning $25/hour vs. $12/hour may be quite small after accounting for lost benefits and increased costs. This does not mean the policy is wrong, but it means the "net positive" claim for individual workers requires careful benefit-cliff modeling that is rarely done in public advocacy.

---

## Summary

The steelman case is genuinely strong: the federal minimum wage has eroded dramatically in real terms, monopsony dynamics suppress wages below competitive levels, and state-level evidence shows higher wages are achievable without catastrophic job losses. A well-designed, phased increase could meaningfully raise living standards for tens of millions of workers.

The red team case is also genuinely strong: $25 is calibrated to high-cost cities, not a national median; automation risk is real and growing; small businesses face existential pressure that large corporations do not; and the empirical literature simply does not provide good evidence about increases of this magnitude. The workers most likely to be harmed are among the most vulnerable.

A rigorous assessment would conclude: a $15–$18 federal minimum with regional adjustment mechanisms has a reasonably strong evidence base behind it. A flat $25 national minimum involves substantially more uncertainty, and the risk that it harms the specific low-income workers it is designed to help is non-trivial rather than dismissible.
